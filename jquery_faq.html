National Park FAQs:

Park Service Created in 1916
President Woodrow Wilson signed the “Organic Act”, creating the National Park Service,in August 1916.
Teddy Roosevelt doubled their Number
Theodore Roosevelt doubled the number of parks within the National Park system while President from 1901 to 1909. HE Also signed the Antiquities Act of June 8, 1906, Enabling himself and future Presidents to proclaim historic landmarks, structures, and objects of historic or scientific interest as national monuments.
The Biggest Living Things!
National parks are home to life on a big scale: Sequoia trees are some of the world's largest living things, and Alaskan brown bears are some of the world's largest carnivores.
Highest Peak
Mount McKinley in the Alaska Range of Denali National Park is the highest point in the national parks at 20,302 feet.
Deepest Cave
Carlsbad Caverns National Park in New Mexico is home to the nation's deepest cave, which is 1,593 feet deep.
Deepest Lake
At 1,932 feet deep, Crater Lake National Park in Oregon is the deepest lake in the U.S.
Old Yeller
Yellowstone National Park (in Wyoming, Idaho, and Montana) is the oldest U.S. national park, founded in 1872.
Wondrous
The Grand Canyon, in Grand Canyon National Park in Arizona, is known as one of the Seven Natural Wonders of the World.
Take Care of your feet
There are over 18,000 miles of trails in the national parks! This includes 2,000 miles of the Appalachian National Scenic Trail that stretches from Maine to Georgia.
Too Cold!
Alaska is home to one of the least visited national parks: Kobuk Valley National Park receives just 3,000 visitors per year, and lies within the Arctic Circle
